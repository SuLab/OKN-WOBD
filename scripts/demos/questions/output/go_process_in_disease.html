## Multi-Layer Analysis of Extracellular Matrix Dysregulation in Pulmonary Fibrosis This comprehensive analysis identified significant dysregulation of extracellular matrix organization genes in pulmonary fibrosis through integration of knowledge graphs, single-cell RNA sequencing, and bulk transcriptomic validation. From 30 genes annotated to the GO term "extracellular matrix organization" (GO:0030198), 8 showed tissue-level upregulation in fibrotic lungs compared to normal controls. The most dramatically upregulated genes were **MMP7** (122.6-fold increase), **FGG** (71.3-fold), **TNC** (19.8-fold), **FGA** (11.8-fold), and **COL4A1** (6.2-fold). These findings represent tissue-level maximal fold changes, meaning at least one cell type within the lung showed this magnitude of upregulation, though individual cell populations may exhibit distinct patterns. **Pulmonary alveolar type 2 cells emerged as the primary cellular driver** of extracellular matrix dysregulation in pulmonary fibrosis. This cell type showed upregulation of all 8 identified genes, including key matrix metalloproteinases (MMP7, MMP23B), collagens (COL4A1, COL4A2, COL13A1), and matrix glycoproteins (TNC). Notably, type 2 pneumocytes also showed downregulation of 9 other matrix organization genes, indicating complex remodeling rather than uniform upregulation. The analysis revealed 17 total matrix-related genes with altered expression in this cell type, suggesting these epithelial cells undergo substantial reprogramming of their matrix interaction profile during fibrotic progression. **Bulk RNA-seq validation across 10 independent studies strongly confirmed the single-cell findings**, with all 8 upregulated genes showing consistent elevation in fibrotic samples. The validation demonstrated robust effect sizes: TNC (64.3-fold, log2FC=6.01), COL4A1 (21.8-fold), COL4A2 (21.7-fold), and MMP7 (15.4-fold) showed the strongest upregulation patterns. Importantly, while FGG showed the second-highest fold change in single-cell analysis (71.3x), it demonstrated more modest but still significant upregulation in bulk samples (2.4-fold), suggesting this gene may be highly cell-type specific or represent a subpopulation effect. These findings illuminate the molecular basis of aberrant matrix remodeling in pulmonary fibrosis, with type 2 pneumocytes serving as key orchestrators of pathological matrix deposition and degradation. The upregulation of both matrix-degrading enzymes (MMP7) and structural components (collagens, fibrinogen chains) suggests a dysregulated wound healing response. Data integration across CellxGene Census single-cell atlases and ARCHS4 bulk repositories (including studies GSE73189, GSE79544, GSE83501, GSE83505, GSE92592) provides robust evidence for these extracellular matrix organization genes as potential therapeutic targets in pulmonary fibrosis.
